Henri Rousseau

Henri Rousseau (1844–1910) was a French Post-Impressionist painter celebrated for his Naïve or Primitive style. Often called Le Douanier (the customs officer), Rousseau worked as a toll collector before turning to art full-time in his late 40s, having started painting seriously only a few years earlier.
Largely self-taught, Rousseau was mocked by critics early on but eventually recognized as a visionary whose unique style influenced many modern artists. His work is marked by vivid colors, flat perspectives, and imaginative scenes—especially his famous jungle paintings, such as Tiger in a Tropical Storm (Surprised!), despite never having left France. He gathered inspiration from botanical gardens, zoo visits, and illustrated books.
Rousseau exhibited frequently at the Salon des Indépendants beginning in 1886. His painting The Sleeping Gypsy (1897) is one of his most iconic works, depicting a mysterious figure in a dreamlike desert scene. In 1905, his jungle-themed painting The Hungry Lion was shown alongside works by the Fauves, earning him renewed attention.
Though he lived modestly in Paris, Rousseau’s creativity, originality, and determination helped secure his legacy. Today, he is regarded as a key figure who paved the way for modernist movements and inspired generations of avant-garde artists.
