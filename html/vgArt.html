The Starry Night

The Starry Night, painted by Vincent van Gogh in June 1889, captures a dreamy night sky from his asylum room in Saint-Rémy-de-Provence. Featuring swirling stars and a tranquil village, the artwork blends reality with imagination. Today, it remains a celebrated masterpiece housed in New York’s Museum of Modern Art.

The Olive Trees

In 1889, while living in an asylum in Saint-Rémy-de-Provence, Vincent van Gogh painted at least 15 olive tree works. Fascinated by their shifting colors and forms, he used them to explore symbolism, spirituality, and nature. These paintings reflected his emotional struggles, yet remain some of his most admired creations.

Portrait of the Postman Joseph Roulin

Vincent van Gogh painted The Roulin Family in Arles during 1888–1889, capturing Joseph Roulin, his wife Augustine, and their three children. The series is notable for its emotional depth and artistic creativity. Van Gogh valued their friendship and used vivid colors and imagination to portray each family member’s unique stage of life.

Sorrow

Vincent van Gogh’s Sorrow (1882) features Clasina Maria Hoornik, a pregnant woman and frequent model. Created early in his career, the drawing is considered a masterwork of draftsmanship. Van Gogh valued the emotional honesty of the piece, calling it a heartfelt beginning and one of his finest figure studies.

Girl with a Mandolin

Girl with a Mandolin is a 1910 Cubist painting by Pablo Picasso, created in Paris. It is one of his early Analytic Cubist works and is held by the Museum of Modern Art in New York. Picasso considered it unfinished, revealing his blend of abstraction with physical reality.

Boy Leading a Horse

Boy Leading a Horse shows a nude boy guiding a horse without reins, using his clenched fist. Painted by Picasso in muted browns and greys, it was originally part of a larger planned composition called The Watering Place. Although abandoned, studies and sketches of the full scene survive.

Three Musicians

Three Musicians is a large oil painting by Pablo Picasso, created in 1921 near Paris. There are two versions, both in Synthetic Cubist style, featuring three masked figures dressed as Pierrot, Harlequin, and a monk. The darker version is more famous, and one includes a dog under the table.

The Young Ladies of Avignon

Les Demoiselles d'Avignon (1907) by Pablo Picasso depicts five nude prostitutes with sharp, angular forms, blending Iberian, African, and Egyptian influences. This groundbreaking work rejected traditional perspective, sparking Cubism’s rise and marking Picasso as a pivotal figure in modern art’s evolution during the 20th century.

Tiger in a Tropical Storm (Surprised!)

Tiger in a Tropical Storm or Surprised! (1891) by Henri Rousseau shows a tiger ready to pounce amid a storm, highlighted by lightning. Despite mixed reviews and initial ridicule for its naïve style, the painting’s technical skill impressed some, making it a landmark work in Rousseau’s jungle-themed series.

The Dream

The Dream (1910) by Henri Rousseau is his final and largest jungle-themed painting. It shows Yadwigha, his Polish mistress, reclining naked on a divan, surrounded by lush jungle plants and exotic animals. The surreal scene blends imagination with nature, highlighted by a mysterious snake charmer under moonlight.

The Sleeping Gypsy

The Sleeping Gypsy (1897) by Henri Rousseau is a dreamlike oil painting showing a lion quietly observing a sleeping woman beneath the moonlight. This fantastical scene captures a serene moment of nature and mystery. It is part of the Museum of Modern Art’s collection in New York, displayed near Van Gogh’s The Starry Night.

The Hungry Lion Throws Itself on the Antelope

The Hungry Lion Throws Itself on the Antelope (1905) by Henri Rousseau depicts a dramatic jungle scene with a lion biting an antelope’s neck under a red sunset. Surrounded by dense foliage, a panther, owl with prey, and a lurking ape-like figure add to the wild atmosphere. Rousseau drew inspiration from museum dioramas.

I and the Village

I and the Village” (1911) by Marc Chagall is a Cubist-inspired oil painting featuring a green-faced man and a goat surrounded by symbolic images like a glowing tree and an upside-down violinist. It reflects Chagall’s childhood memories and Eastern European folklore in a whimsical style. The painting is at the Museum of Modern Art in New York.

Calvary

Chagall’s Golgotha blends Russian icon influence with his unique style, portraying Christ not as crucified but as a blue child symbolizing resurrection and salvation. The painting reflects his vision of suffering, love, and hope, inspired by religious themes and personal experiences during times of persecution.

The Drunkard

Chagall’s The Drunkard (1911-12) evolved from a detailed gouache to an abstract oil painting. It replaces a realistic interior with a patterned plane, simplified objects, and vivid colors. The work’s magic lies in its ornamental surface and vibrant contrasts, reflecting a poetic, psychological reality like I and the Village.

The Fiddler

Marc Chagall’s The Fiddler (1913) blends Russian and French styles in vivid contrast. It depicts a fiddler symbolizing life’s key moments—birth, wedding, death—and the inner struggles of the individual. Rooted in Chagall’s nostalgia and cultural heritage, the violinist reflects the spiritual power of music and dance in his village.
